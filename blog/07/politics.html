Climate change is a global problem. In order to face it, all countries have to work together.

“Distribution of global emissions reinforces the need for broad multilateral cooperation in mitigating climate change. Fifteen to twenty countries are responsible for roughly 75 percent of global emissions, but no one country accounts for more than about 26 percent. Efforts to cut emissions—mitigation—must therefore be global. Without international cooperation and coordination, some states may free ride on others' efforts, or even exploit uneven emissions controls to gain competitive advantage. And because the impacts of climate change will be felt around the world, efforts to adapt to climate change—adaptation—will need to be global too.” (1)

Efforts have been made in an international agreement on climate change. The 2015 Paris UN Climate Change Conference is such an example. 196 countries met to negotiate the Paris Agreement, in which the goal was to limit the increase of global temperatures to 2 degree Celsius. While it was a historic and meaningful moment in which a very large amount of countries made an effort to face the problem of climate change together, the Agreement is deemed too unrealistic by many. It did, however, create a precedent and reinforce the idea that countries can and should cooperate on this important issue.

It is difficult for developing countries to cut down on carbon emissions, since they often do not have the resources or infrastructure needed to do so. Is it thus unreasonable to expect the same amount of effort in terms of environmental action from those countries?

Another problem impedes heavily on environmental efforts: some do not believe in climate change, or do not believe it is a priority. A recent example is with the Trump administration. “In a sharp break with scientific consensus, the new administrator of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency says that carbon dioxide’s role in the Earth’s changing climate remains unclear.” (2).

Despite the wealth of scientific research and general consensus in the scientific community, Trump has proposed heavy cuts in environmental programs. If an influential country like the USA continues down this path, it could lead to other countries decreasing their environmental efforts in favor for economic growth instead. It is a very dangerous possible future. Governments play a huge role in countries’ policies and approaches to climate change, and we must do what we can to hold them responsible.
